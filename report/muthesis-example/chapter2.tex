\chapter{Background and Related Work}
\label{cha:background}

This chapter introduces the relevant background and related work for this project, structured around its key design decisions. It starts by framing HPC support as an information retrieval and question answering problem, then introduces the concept of \ac{RAG} and its relevance to this domain. The chapter also discusses the heterogeneous nature of support corpora, including documentation and tickets, and the challenges of authority, freshness, and validity in retrieval. Finally, it reviews existing evaluation methodologies for RAG systems, identifies common failure modes of naive retrieval approaches, and positions this research within the broader landscape of related work.

\section{HPC Support as an Information Retrieval and Question Answering Problem}
\label{sec:support}

Current user support for HPC systems such as \ac{CSD3} is typically delivered through a combination of documentation, helpdesk systems, and direct communication with support staff and \ac{HPC} experts \citep{AskHPC}. This support ecosystem is underpinned by a large body of user documentation, which serves as a first point of reference for users and helps them solve problems independently. However, much of this essential information is scattered across fragmented, multimodal sources, making it difficult and time-consuming to locate. \citet{AskHPC} therefore argue that as \ac{HPC} systems and software stacks continue to grow in complexity, it becomes increasingly difficult for even experienced users to navigate this documentation effectively, creating barriers to usability and causing frustration, errors, and reduced productivity \citep{AskHPC}. This is particularly important in the context of \ac{HPC} support, where incorrect guidance can result in misconfigurations, failed computations, wasted computational resources, or even violations of facility policies \citep{AskHPC}. 

These characteristics make \ac{HPC} support a natural \ac{IR} and \ac{QA} problem \cite{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,AskHPC}. In the technical support setting, answering a user's query is a two-stage process: the system must first retrieve the relevant documents or support artefacts, and the retrieved information must then be read to identify the answer \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. Specifically, the retrieved information must not only be semantically similar to the user's query, but it must also contain the required information to answer the question \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. This is particularly important for procedural and troubleshooting questions, for which the answer is non-factoid and often requires actionable guidance rather than a memorised fact \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}. The task is further complicated by the fact that the required documentation may not exist, and even if it does, it may be difficult to find in a fragmented, multimodal documentation environment \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,AskHPC}. 

Technical support \ac{QA} differs from general open-domain \ac{QA} in several key ways \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. First, it is grounded in real user queries that use specialised domain language and often refer to specific software versions, configurations, and snippets of stack traces \citep{castelliTechQADataset2020}. Second, \citet{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021} argue that, in contrast to general open-domain \ac{QA}, technical support \ac{QA} often contains a weak lexical overlap between the question and the answer. Therefore, documents that appear semantically relevant may fail to contain the actual resolution. Straightforward retrieve-then-read pipelines may return such documents, making them more error-prone in technical settings \cite{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. Third, technical \ac{QA} is constrained by relatively small labelled datasets, since annotation requires domain expertise and is significantly more expensive than crowd-sourced open-domain annotation \cite{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}. Taken together, these characteristics make direct transfer from methods trained on open-domain corpora more difficult and can lead to weaker performance in technical settings \cite{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}.

In addition to official documentation, historical support tickets provide another source of operational support knowledge in \ac{HPC} settings \cite{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. TicketHub shows that historical support tickets can help staff identify prior issues and resolutions, improving the efficiency of support responses \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. The authors also show that such tickets play an important role in preserving institutional knowledge, which is particularly valuable when new staff face a steep learning curve or when ticketing systems change. Therefore, \ac{HPC} support depends not only on access to documentation, but also on access to prior support interactions and their resolutions \cite{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. 

Taken together, the literature indicates that \ac{HPC} support is best understood as a specialised, evidence-dependent form of technical support \ac{QA} rather than as a general instance of open-domain factoid \ac{QA} \cite{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020,AskHPC}. 

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Generation}
\label{sec:rag}

As discussed in \ref{sec:support}, technical support \ac{QA} is evidence-dependent: answering a query requires retrieving the relevant external information and identifying an answer within it \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}. For this reason, parametric-only \ac{LM}s are a weak fit for knowledge-intensive support tasks, because they rely on knowledge stored in model parameters rather than directly verifiable external evidence \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}. As \citet{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020} note, such models are difficult to update, provide limited provenance for their predictions, and can hallucinate on knowledge-intensive tasks. Retrieval-augmented generation (\acs{RAG}) addresses this limitation by combining language generation with retrieval from an external knowledge source \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}.

\citet{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020} define \ac{RAG} as a hybrid architecture that combines parametric memory in a pre-trained sequence-to-sequence generator model with non-parametric memory in a dense document index. At inference time, a neural retriever selects the most relevant passages for a given input query and the generator produces a response using these passages as additional context. \ac{RAG} is therefore a retrieval-grounded generation framework intended to improve factual performance while making the evidential basis of an answer more inspectable \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}.

\citet{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020} report strong empirical results on open-domain \ac{QA}, knowledge-intensive generation, and fact verification benchmarks. Their models outperform parametric-only baselines on several open-domain \ac{QA} tasks and generate more factual, specific, and diverse responses than BART in generation settings \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}. Additionally, because knowledge is stored partly in an external document index, that knowledge can be updated by replacing or rebuilding the index rather than retraining the generator. Related work further shows that retrieval-augmented generation can also reduce hallucination in grounded conversation tasks \citep{shusterRetrievalAugmentationReduces2021}.

However, the original \ac{RAG} architecture does not by itself ensure that retrieval is necessary, that the retrieved material is trustworthy, or that the generator will faithfully use it in the final answer \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. \citet{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023} argue that fixed-$k$ retrieval can be wasteful or harmful when retrieval is unnecessary or when the retrieved passages are weakly grounded. \citet{liYouKnowWhat2024} show that \ac{RAG} quality depends on query-knowledge relevance, and that it degrades when the underlying corpus is out of scope or out of date. \citet{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025} further show that relevance alone is insufficient in multi-source settings, because source reliability affects whether retrieved evidence should be trusted. For technical support, this means that semantically relevant material may still be unusable if it is outdated or if it comes from a less reliable source \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}.

Taken together, these studies shift the design problem from simply adding a retrieval component to controlling when retrieval is used and which evidence is prioritised \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. Self-RAG is one response to this problem. \citet{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023} propose a framework that allows retrieval on demand and uses reflection tokens to assess the relevance of retrieved passages and whether the generated content is supported by the retrieved evidence.

Applied support-orientated \ac{RAG} systems also suggest that retrieval design remains domain-specific. AskHPC combines curated \ac{HPC} documentation with specialised parsing \citep{AskHPC}, while customer-service systems have incorporated structured knowledge graphs to improve retrieval \cite{LinkedInRAG}. For \ac{HPC} support, the implication is that a useful \ac{RAG} system must not only retrieve semantically relevant information, but it must also prioritise evidence that is current, trustworthy, and operationally valid \citep{AskHPC,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. This motivates this project's focus on validity-aware retrieval over heterogeneous support evidence, rather on generation alone. 

\section{Heterogeneous Support Corpora: Documentation and Tickets}
\label{sec:heterogeneous}

\ac{HPC} user support depends on evidence drawn from multiple support corpora, including local institutional documentation, external technical documentation, and historical support tickets, rather than from a single homogeneous text collection \citep{AskHPC,rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. These corpora should not be treated as interchangeable, because they differ in authority, scope, and temporal reliability, and those differences affect what counts as valid support evidence \cite{AskHPC,rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. Therefore, the choice of source is itself part of the retrieval problem. 

Local official documentation should be treated as the primary documentary source for \ac{HPC} support because it records the workflows, constraints, and configurations that are actually supported by the facility \citep{AskHPC,ResearchComputingServicesDocumentation}. This is especially important in \ac{HPC} setting, where such documentation is often the first point of reference of users \citep{AskHPC}. In the case of \ac{CSD3}, this official corpus covers core support topics such as access, authentication, job submission, software environments, and local policy \cite{ResearchComputingServicesDocumentation}. 

Official local documentation forms the primary documentary corpus for \ac{HPC} support because it serves as the first point of reference for users and records the supported workflows and configurations of the facility \citep{AskHPC}. In practice, such documentation typically covers core support topics such as access, job submission, software environments, storage, debugging, and system-specific procedures \citep{AskHPC,ResearchComputingServicesDocumentation}. Local documentation is not only important because it covers a broad range of support topics, but also because it it has local authority, reflecting the supported procedures and configurations of the platform rather than general technical advice \citep{AskHPC,ResearchComputingServicesDocumentation}. However, \citet{AskHPC} note that this documentation is often fragmented across user guides, technical manuals, policy pages, and other sources, making it difficult for users to locate the relevant guidance efficiently \citep{AskHPC}. Within this project, official \ac{CSD3} documentation \citep{ResearchComputingServicesDocumentation} is therefore treated as the primary source of locally supported usage. 

External technical documentation provides a complementary form of documentary evidence, particularly where support queries depend on software- or tool-specific detail that is not fully covered in the official local documentation \citep{AskHPC}. The role of external documentation is illustrated by AskHPC, which integrates facility user guides with scheduler documentation and programming-model documentation in a single knowledge base to "support queries that may need information retrieved from multiple sources" \citep{AskHPC}. External documentation is also used more broadly within technical support \ac{QA}. TechQA pairs real user questions with answers grounded in formal technical documents, showing that support questions often require retrieval over specialised corpora rather than conversational evidence alone \citep{castelliTechQADataset2020}. Within this project, external documentation is therefore treated as a supplementary source of evidence that can provide useful software-specific detail, but is not definitive for local operational truth.

In contrast to documentation, historical support tickets provide concrete records of prior issues, resolutions, and support interactions \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025}. In HPC settings, ticket analysis has been shown to help staff identify common problems, locate previous resolutions more efficiently, and retain operational knowledge that might otherwise remain distributed across individuals or legacy systems \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. Furthermore, more recent work shows that ticket corpora can be transformed into structured issue-resolution representations and used to generate recurrent support knowledge, such as automated FAQ generation \citep{joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025}. On the other hand, ticket evidence is typically noisier and less standardised than formal documentation, since it consists of conversational problem-solving, which may be repetitive, incomplete, or inconsistent \citep{joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025}. Therefore, the use of tickets within this project is valuable as they preserve case-specific operational context, even though that same context makes the tickets more difficult to interpret and more sensitive to changes in the underlying system \cite{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025}.

Taken together, official documentation, external documentation, and historical tickets provide complementary but non-interchangeable sources of support evidence: the documentary sources provide formal guidance and technical reference material, while the historical tickets preserve prior issue-resolution interactions and case-specific operational context \citep{AskHPC,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025,rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. In this project, these differences mean that the heterogeneous support evidence cannot be treated as a single undifferentiated text pool. Instead, the appropriateness of the source itself becomes part of the retrieval problem, motivating the importance of authority, freshness, and validity in retrieval design.
