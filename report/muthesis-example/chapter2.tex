\chapter{Background and Related Work}
\label{cha:background}

This chapter reviews the literature needed to justify the project's core design choices. It first frames \ac{HPC} support as a specialised retrieval-grounded question-answering task, then reviews retrieval-augmented generation, heterogeneous support evidence, authority and freshness in retrieval, and methods for evaluating \ac{RAG} systems. It concludes by identifying the specific gap addressed by this dissertation. 

\section{HPC Support as an Information Retrieval and Question Answering Problem}
\label{sec:support}

\ac{HPC} user support is typically delivered through a mixture of documentation, helpdesk systems, and direct interaction with support staff. In practice, the relevant guidance is often fragmented across local facility documentation, scheduler manuals, software documentation, and prior support exchanges, making it difficult for users to locate the evidence they need to solve operational problems \citep{AskHPC}. As \ac{HPC} environments grow more complex, this fragmentation increases both user difficulty and support demand. In this setting, the cost of error is also high, since incorrect advice can lead to misconfiguration, failed jobs, wasted compute time, or policy violations \citep{AskHPC}.

These characteristics make \ac{HPC} support a specialised information-retrieval and question-answering problem rather than a generic conversational one \citep{AskHPC,yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. A useful system must retrieve evidence that not only appears relevant but actually contains the information needed to resolve the query. Unlike many open-domain \ac{QA} tasks, support questions are often procedural, version-sensitive, and expressed with weak lexical overlap between the query and the relevant resolution, so seemingly relevant passages may still fail to support a correct answer \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}. Technical-support datasets are also relatively scarce because expert annotation is expensive, which makes direct transfer from open-domain \ac{QA} less reliable \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}.

In \ac{HPC} settings, the evidence base extends beyond formal documentation. Historical support tickets preserve prior cases, diagnostic context, and previously successful resolutions, and can therefore act as an additional operational knowledge source for staff-assisted support \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025}. As a whole, the literature supports treating \ac{HPC} support as evidence-dependent technical \ac{QA} over a fragmented and heterogeneous corpus rather than as a pure language-generation problem. 

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Generation}
\label{sec:rag}

Retrieval-augmented generation (\ac{RAG}) is a natural fit for technical support because it combines language generation with access to external evidence rather than relying only on parametric model memory \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}. In its original form, a retriever selects passages from an indexed corpus and a generator produces an answer conditioned on those passages. For support settings, this is attractive for two reasons. Firstly, the evidence base can be updated without retraining the generator \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}. Secondly, the evidential basis of an answer can be inspected by human reviewers before it is acted upon \citep{shusterRetrievalAugmentationReduces2021}. 

However, baseline \ac{RAG} does not guarantee that retrieval is necessary, that the retrieved passages are trustworthy, or that the generator will use them faithfully. Later work shows that performance degrades when evidence is weakly relevant, out of scope, outdated, or drawn from less reliable sources \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. For technical support, this matters because semantically relevant material may still be unusable if it is stale or not authoritative for the local environment. 

Recent work has therefore shifted attention from retrieval alone to retrieval control. This includes strategies for identifying when retrieval should occur, whether the corpus is capable of supporting the query, and which sources should be trusted once retrieval takes place \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. These studies show that reliable support \ac{RAG} requires more than semantic matching. It requires the explicit handling of retrieval necessity, corpus scope, and source reliability, but it still leaves unresolved how evidence should be prioritised when documentation and tickets differ in authority, freshness, and local applicability. \citet{AskHPC} demonstrate that support-oriented retrieval pipelines are domain-specific and benefit from carefully curated evidence preparation, yet their system remains primarily documentation-grounded. The open design question for this dissertation is therefore not whether retrieval helps, but how heterogeneous support evidence should be ranked so that the final answer is grounded in the most defensible material for the current \ac{CSD3} context. 

\section{Heterogeneous Support Corpora: Documentation and Tickets}
\label{sec:heterogeneous}

\ac{HPC} support does not operate over a single uniform corpus. It draws on local facility documentation, external technical documentation, and historical support tickets, each of which differs in authority, update cycle, evidential role, and temporal stability \citep{AskHPC,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025,rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. In multi-source retrieval, these differences matter because a passage may be topically relevant yet still be poor evidence for an answer if it is not locally applicable or no longer current. Local documentation is the primary source for current site-specific practice because it records the workflows, constraints, and policies actually supported on the target system \citep{AskHPC,ResearchComputingServicesDocumentation}. External technical documentation plays a complementary role by supplying software-specific detail that local guides may omit \citep{AskHPC}. However, it should not override local operational truth for policy, permissions, or supported configuration \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}.

Historical support tickets contribute a different type of evidence by preserving prior cases, diagnostic context, and previously used resolutions \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025}. They are therefore valuable for troubleshooting and recurring issues, but they are also noisier, more privacy-sensitive, and more vulnerable to temporal drift than documentation. Furthermore, a closed ticket may preserve a once-correct resolution that is no longer valid after changes to software, policy, or system configuration \citep{joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025,liYouKnowWhat2024}.

The literature therefore supports treating these sources as distinct evidential categories rather than flattening them into a single homogeneous corpus. Flattening the corpus would obscure differences in source role, temporal stability, and local authority, which are critical for determining whether a passage should ground an answer. For this project, the retrieval problem is not only to find relevant passages, but also to account for source role, freshness, and local applicability when deciding which evidence should ground the final answer. 

\section{Authority, Freshness, and Validity in Retrieval}
\label{sec:authority-freshness-validity}

In this project, the key retrieval question is not only whether a passage matches the topic of a query, but whether it should count as usable evidence for an answer. In heterogeneous support corpora, relevance-only retrieval is insufficient because highly similar passages may come from less trustworthy sources or may reflect an outdated system state \citep{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,liYouKnowWhat2024}. This is particularly risky in operational support, where outdated evidence can reduce accuracy and mislead generation even when better evidence is also present \citep{ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025}.

Authority, freshness, and validity capture different aspects of this problem. Authority refers to how much a source should be trusted for the type of claim being made. Freshness refers to whether the information still reflects the current state of the system. Validity, as used in this dissertation, refers to evidence that is jointly relevant, authoritative, current, and locally applicable to the present support context. A passage can therefore be topically relevant yet still be invalid if it is stale, non-authoritative, or mismatched to local practice. 

The literature implies that retrieval for \ac{HPC} support should be source-aware and freshness-sensitive rather than organised as a relevance-only search over a flattened corpus \citep{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,liYouKnowWhat2024,ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025}. It also implies that when the available evidence is weak or conflicting, conservative behaviour is preferable to forced resolution \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023,liYouKnowWhat2024}. This motivates the validity-aware retrieval and reranking design developed in Chapter~\ref{cha:system-design-implementation}.

\section{Evaluation of RAG Systems}
\label{sec:evaluation}

\ac{RAG} evaluation is multi-dimensional because end-to-end answer quality depends on both retrieval quality and whether the generator uses the retrieved evidence faithfully \citep{RAGAS,ruRAGCheckerFinegrainedFramework2024}. A poor answer may arise from missing evidence, poorly ranked evidence, or unsupported use of otherwise relevant context. For this reason, recent work treats \ac{RAG} evaluation as a combination of retrieval-oriented and generation-oriented metrics rather than a single headline score \citep{RAGAS,saad-falconARESAutomatedEvaluation2024,ruRAGCheckerFinegrainedFramework2024}.

RAGAS is useful during system development because it provides scalable automated metrics such as faithfulness and context recall without requiring gold standards for every query \citep{RAGAS}. However, automated judge-based metrics are not automatically trustworthy. ARES shows that judge performance depends on calibration and validation, while RAGChecker shows the value of diagnostic evaluation that separates retriever and generator failure modes \citep{saad-falconARESAutomatedEvaluation2024,ruRAGCheckerFinegrainedFramework2024}. In practice, automated scoring is valuable for iterative comparison, but it is not sufficient on its own for strong comparative claims. 

The evaluation literature also suggests that benchmark design matters as much as metric choice \citep{BenchmarkingLargeLanguage}. Useful \ac{RAG} benchmarks should expose failure conditions such as missing evidence, misleading evidence, noisy retrieval, or the need to integrate information across documents \citep{BenchmarkingLargeLanguage}. For this reason, strong \ac{RAG} evaluation combines automated metrics with human-grounded checks and targeted error analysis. This literature justifies the methodology used later in the dissertation, which combines automated metrics for scalable comparison and tuning, manual evaluation for response quality, and error analysis for critical interpretation of the results. 

\section{Gap Analysis and Research Positioning}
\label{sec:gap}

The literature reviewed above establishes three points. First, \ac{HPC} support is best framed as specialised technical \ac{QA} over fragmented evidence rather than as generic chatbot interaction \citep{AskHPC,yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. Second, the relevant evidence base is heterogeneous, with local documentation, external technical documentation, and historical tickets each contributing different types of support knowledge and each differing in authority, freshness, and local applicability \citep{AskHPC,rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. Third, reliable \ac{RAG} depends not only on retrieval relevance but also on the quality and trustworthiness of the retrieved evidence \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025}.

Prior work addresses parts of this problem, but not the full intersection addressed here. \citet{AskHPC} demonstrate the value of an \ac{HPC} assistant grounded in curated documentation, while \citet{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023} and \citet{joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025} show that historical tickets are a valuable source of operational knowledge for support analysis and reuse. Reliability-aware and scope-aware \ac{RAG} studies show why relevance alone is insufficient, but they do not directly address how site-specific documentation and historical tickets should be combined in an \ac{HPC} support setting where local operational truth and temporal drift are critical concerns \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025}.

The gap addressed by this dissertation is therefore specific and testable. In particular, it addresses whether answer quality improves when documentation and ticket evidence are combined and prioritised using validity-aware retrieval rather than source-agnostic semantic ranking. Within the scoped setting of supervised first-response assistance for \ac{CSD3}, the later chapters evaluate this question through separate analyses of retrieval quality, grounded generation, and failure modes rather than relying on a single overall score.
