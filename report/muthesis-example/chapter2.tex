\chapter{Background and Related Work}
\label{cha:background}

This chapter introduces the relevant background and related work for this project, structured around its key design decisions. It starts by framing HPC support as an information retrieval and question answering problem, then introduces the concept of \ac{RAG} and its relevance to this domain. The chapter also discusses the heterogeneous nature of support corpora, including documentation and tickets, and the challenges of authority, freshness, and validity in retrieval. Finally, it reviews existing evaluation methodologies for RAG systems, and positions this research within the broader landscape of related work.

\section{HPC Support as an Information Retrieval and Question Answering Problem}
\label{sec:support}

Current user support for HPC systems such as \ac{CSD3} is typically delivered through a combination of documentation, helpdesk systems, and direct communication with support staff and \ac{HPC} experts \citep{AskHPC}. User documentation is a central component of \ac{HPC} support and often serves as the first point of reference for users \citep{AskHPC}. \citet{AskHPC} show that this guidance is often fragmented across multimodal sources, making relevant information difficult and time-consuming to locate. As \ac{HPC} systems and software stacks continue to grow in complexity, even experienced users may need to integrate guidance from multiple sources, creating barriers to usability and increasing reliance on support channels \citep{AskHPC}. This is particularly important in the context of \ac{HPC} support, where incorrect guidance can result in misconfigurations, failed computations, wasted computational resources, or even violations of facility policies \citep{AskHPC}. 

These characteristics make \ac{HPC} support a natural \ac{IR} and \ac{QA} problem \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,AskHPC}. In the technical support setting, answering a user's query is a two-stage process: the system must first retrieve the relevant documents or support artefacts, and the retrieved information must then be read to identify the answer \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. Specifically, the retrieved information must not only be semantically similar to the user's query, but it must also contain the required information to answer the question \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. This is particularly important for procedural and troubleshooting questions, for which the answer is non-factoid and often requires actionable guidance rather than a memorised fact \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}. The task is further complicated because the required documentation may be missing or difficult to locate in fragmented support environments \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,AskHPC}. 

Technical support \ac{QA} differs from general open-domain \ac{QA} in several key ways \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. First, it is grounded in real user queries that often refer to products, versions, configurations, commands, error messages, and stack traces \citep{castelliTechQADataset2020}. Second, \citet{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021} argue that, in contrast to general open-domain \ac{QA}, technical support \ac{QA} often contains a weak lexical overlap between the question and the answer. Therefore, documents that appear semantically relevant may fail to contain the actual resolution. Straightforward retrieve-then-read pipelines may return such documents, making them more error-prone in technical settings \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. Third, technical \ac{QA} is constrained by relatively small labelled datasets, since annotation requires domain expertise and is significantly more expensive than crowd-sourced open-domain annotation \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}. Taken together, these characteristics make direct transfer from methods trained on open-domain corpora more difficult and can lead to weaker performance in technical settings \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}.

In addition to local documentation, historical support tickets provide another source of operational support knowledge in \ac{HPC} settings \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. TicketHub shows that historical support tickets can help staff identify prior issues and resolutions, improving the efficiency of support responses \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. The authors further show that improved access to archival tickets helps preserve operational knowledge and reduces the learning burden on newer staff, especially when institutional systems or workflows change. Therefore, \ac{HPC} support depends not only on access to documentation, but also on access to prior support interactions and their resolutions \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. 

Overall, the literature supports treating \ac{HPC} support as a specialised form of technical \ac{QA} in which answers depend on retrieving operationally usable evidence rather than recalling isolated facts \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020,AskHPC}. 

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Generation}
\label{sec:rag}

As discussed in \ref{sec:support}, technical support \ac{QA} is evidence-dependent: answering a query requires retrieving the relevant external information and identifying an answer within it \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}. For this reason, parametric-only \ac{LM}s are a weak fit for knowledge-intensive support tasks, because they rely on knowledge stored in model parameters rather than directly verifiable external evidence \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}. As \citet{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020} note, such models are difficult to update, provide limited provenance for their predictions, and can hallucinate on knowledge-intensive tasks. Retrieval-augmented generation (\acs{RAG}) addresses this limitation by combining language generation with retrieval from an external knowledge source \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}.

\citet{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020} define \ac{RAG} as a hybrid architecture that combines parametric memory in a pre-trained sequence-to-sequence generator model with non-parametric memory in a dense document index. At inference time, a neural retriever selects the most relevant passages for a given input query and the generator produces a response using these passages as additional context. \ac{RAG} is therefore a retrieval-grounded generation framework intended to improve factual performance while making the evidential basis of an answer more inspectable \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}.

\citet{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020} report strong empirical results on open-domain \ac{QA}, knowledge-intensive generation, and fact verification benchmarks. Their models outperform parametric-only baselines on several open-domain \ac{QA} tasks and generate more factual, specific, and diverse responses than BART in generation settings \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}. Additionally, because knowledge is stored partly in an external document index, that knowledge can be updated by replacing or rebuilding the index rather than retraining the generator. Related work further shows that retrieval-augmented generation can reduce hallucination in grounded conversation settings \citep{shusterRetrievalAugmentationReduces2021}.

However, the original \ac{RAG} architecture does not by itself ensure that retrieval is necessary, that the retrieved material is trustworthy, or that the generator will faithfully use it in the final answer \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. \citet{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023} argue that fixed-$k$ retrieval can be wasteful or harmful when retrieval is unnecessary or when the retrieved passages are weakly grounded. \citet{liYouKnowWhat2024} show that \ac{RAG} quality depends on query-knowledge relevance, and that it degrades when the underlying corpus is out of scope or out of date. \citet{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025} further show that relevance alone is insufficient in multi-source settings, because source reliability affects whether retrieved evidence should be trusted. For technical support, this means that semantically relevant material may still be unusable if it is outdated or if it comes from a less reliable source \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}.

Taken together, these studies show that the key design problem is no longer whether to retrieve, but how retrieval should be controlled and which evidence should be prioritised \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. These control strategies operate at different points in the pipeline: Self-RAG controls whether retrieval is necessary at all \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023}, \citet{liYouKnowWhat2024} assess whether the corpus is capable of supporting a query, and \citet{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025} control which sources should be trusted once retrieval occurs. Together they suggest that reliable support \ac{RAG} requires more than stronger semantic matching. It also requires explicit control over retrieval necessity, corpus scope, and source reliability. Applied support-oriented \ac{RAG} systems also suggest that retrieval design remains domain-specific. AskHPC, for example, combines curated \ac{HPC} documentation with specialised parsing \citep{AskHPC}, while customer-service systems have incorporated structured knowledge graphs to improve retrieval \citep{LinkedInRAG}. For \ac{HPC} support, the implication is that a useful \ac{RAG} system must not only retrieve semantically relevant information, but must also prioritise evidence that is current, trustworthy, and operationally valid \citep{AskHPC,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. This motivates this project's focus on evidence prioritisation over heterogeneous support sources, rather than treating generation quality as the primary research problem. 

\section{Heterogeneous Support Corpora: Documentation and Tickets}
\label{sec:heterogeneous}

\ac{HPC} user support draws on a heterogeneous evidence base that includes local facility documentation, external technical documentation, and historical support tickets rather than a single uniform corpus \citep{AskHPC,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025,rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. In this context, heterogeneity means that these sources differ not only in topic coverage, but also in their evidential role, authority, update cycle, and temporal stability \citep{AskHPC,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. In multi-source retrieval settings, these differences affect whether apparently relevant evidence is still trustworthy, locally applicable, and suitable for the current system state \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. Source selection is therefore an important part of the retrieval problem \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. For facility-specific support, local documentation is the primary documentary source because it records the workflows, constraints, and configurations that are actually supported on the target system \citep{AskHPC,ResearchComputingServicesDocumentation}. AskHPC describes user documentation as the first point of reference for \ac{HPC} users, which makes the quality, coverage, and maintenance of local documentation central to scalable support \citep{AskHPC}. In the case of \ac{CSD3}, the local documentation covers access, authentication, job submission, software environments, and site policies \citep{ResearchComputingServicesDocumentation}. It is therefore the best available source for questions about permitted or supported local practice. 

External technical documentation has a complementary evidential role because it can extend local support coverage without displacing local authority \citep{AskHPC}. In multi-source \ac{RAG}, however, such material still raises source-reliability and local-applicability questions \citep{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,liYouKnowWhat2024}. It is most useful when a query depends on tool behaviour, syntax, API usage, or configuration detail that local documentation omits, summarises only briefly, or abstracts away \citep{AskHPC}. \citet{AskHPC} illustrate this multi-source setting clearly by combining facility guides with scheduler manuals and programming-model documentation to answer queries whose evidence requirements span multiple sources. However, general software documentation cannot by itself establish local operational truth, because current local practice may differ from the general case \citep{AskHPC,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. As a result, this project treats external documentation as supplementary evidence for \ac{CSD3}-specific queries rather than as the deciding source on local policy, permissions, or configuration.

Historical support tickets perform a different evidential role to documentation, because they preserve concrete user problems, diagnostic context, troubleshooting paths, and prior resolutions in a way that formal documentation often does not \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025}. \citet{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023} show that archival ticket analysis can help staff identify common issues, retrieve previous resolutions, and reduce the steep learning curve faced by new staff by making prior cases easier to search. \citet{joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025} further show that \ac{HPC} tickets can be normalised into structured issue-resolution pairs and used to generate FAQ-style support knowledge from recurring support patterns. They also note that ticket corpora are difficult to prepare cleanly because they contain domain-specific terminology, cryptic error messages, inconsistent wording, and user-specific artefacts, all of which complicate normalisation, summarisation, and retrieval. Tickets are also a risky evidence source because they are a lagging indicator of system state. While a closed ticket may preserve a useful resolution, that resolution may no longer be valid after later changes to documentation, software versions, or local policy \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,liYouKnowWhat2024}. \citet{joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025} additionally highlight the privacy and governance constraints of ticket data, since personally identifying and user-specific content may persist even after redaction. Accordingly, ticket evidence often requires stronger access control, heavier preprocessing, and more careful freshness checks that documentation sources \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025}.

The same passage may be relevant across documentation, external manuals, and historical tickets, but its evidential status changes with source role, local applicability, and temporal currency. For this project, this means indexing and ranking account for source type, local applicability, freshness, and evidential risk alongside semantic relevance \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. This motivates the validity-aware retrieval design developed in the next section.  

\section{Authority, Freshness, and Validity in Retrieval}
\label{sec:authority-freshness-validity}

Accordingly, the central retrieval question in this project is not simply whether a passage is semantically related to a query, but whether it should be treated as usable evidence for an answer \citep{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,liYouKnowWhat2024}. In a multi-source support corpus, relevance-only retrieval is inadequate because a highly relevant document may come from an unreliable source, or a seemingly appropriate corpus may still fail if the query lies beyond its effective scope or depends on outdated knowledge \citep{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,liYouKnowWhat2024}. This is especially problematic in operational support settings, where outdated information can reduce response accuracy and even steer generation towards harmful answers despite the simultaneous presence of current evidence \citep{ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025}. Here, authority refers to the trustworthiness of a source, freshness to the temporal currency of its information, and validity to the suitability of retrieved evidence for the current local support context.

Authority becomes a retrieval variable once the corpus varies not only in topic coverage but also in evidential trustworthiness \citep{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. \citet{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025} show that standard multi-source \ac{RAG} can retrieve incorrect information because it relies on relevance alone while overlooking source reliability. This can allow unreliable sources to dominate the retrieval process. As discussed in Section \ref{sec:heterogeneous}, this is especially important for operational support questions about local policy, permissions, and supported configuration, where generic software documentation cannot by itself establish local operational truth. Authority is therefore a retrieval problem, not just a post-hoc verification problem: if authoritative evidence is not prioritised before generation, the model may still ground its answer in semantically relevant but less trustworthy material \citep{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}.

Freshness is important because the evidential value of a retrieved document can decay over time, even when its semantic relevance remains high \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025}. \citet{liYouKnowWhat2024} show that \ac{RAG} performance degrades when the underlying corpus is out of date or no longer adequately supports the active query distribution. \citet{ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025} further demonstrate that the presence of outdated information in retrieved context can substantially reduce answer accuracy despite the simultaneous presence of current evidence. In \ac{HPC} support, this temporal risk is amplified because the operational environment itself changes over time, as software stacks, documentation, policies, and even the available hardware evolve, meaning that older resolutions may no longer reflect the current system state \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025,CSD3UpgradeOctober}. Therefore, freshness is not only a useful ranking heuristic, but also a condition on whether retrieved evidence can still be safely used to answer present-focused operational queries \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025}.

In this dissertation, validity is used as an operational term for retrieved evidence that is not only semantically relevant, but also authoritative, current, and locally applicable. This means that a passage can be topically relevant yet still be invalid if it comes from a less trustworthy source, reflects an outdated system state, or does not establish the correct local operational truth \citep{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,liYouKnowWhat2024}. This suggests that temporal validity should not be reduced to a simple age threshold alone, since the usability of support evidence depends on whether it still reflects the current local operational context. It also explains why indiscriminate fixed-$k$ retrieval is risky in this setting: adding more semantically related context does not guarantee better grounding, and the generator is not guaranteed to follow the retrieved evidence faithfully \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023}. Validity-aware retrieval should therefore prefer evidence that is jointly relevant, reliable, current, and applicable to the local support context, rather than treating semantic similarity alone as sufficient ranking criteria \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,liYouKnowWhat2024}.

Taken together, these studies imply that retrieval for HPC support should not be organised as a relevance-only search over a flattened corpus. Instead, it should incorporate source-aware preferences, freshness-sensitive controls, and local applicability constraints so that generation is grounded primarily in evidence that is defensible in the current support context \citep{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,liYouKnowWhat2024,ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025}. They also suggest that when retrieved evidence is weak, stale, or conflicting, conservative response behaviour is preferable to forced resolution \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023,liYouKnowWhat2024}. Therefore, this project treats validity-aware retrieval as a prerequisite for trustworthy support generation, which motivates the retrieval and reranking design developed in Chapter \ref{cha:system-design-implementation}.

\section{Evaluation of RAG Systems}
\label{sec:evaluation}

Evaluating \ac{RAG} systems is more complex than evaluating standalone \ac{LM}s because quality depends both on what evidence is retrieved and on how faithfully the generator uses that evidence \citep{RAGAS,ruRAGCheckerFinegrainedFramework2024}. A single end-to-end score is therefore insufficient because a poor final answer could be caused by retrieval failure, incomplete evidence, or weak use of otherwise valid context \citep{ruRAGCheckerFinegrainedFramework2024}. As a result, recent work treats \ac{RAG} evaluation as a multi-dimensional problem that separates retrieval quality from grounded generation quality \citep{saad-falconARESAutomatedEvaluation2024,RAGAS}. This is typically operationalised through measures such as context relevance, answer faithfulness, and answer relevance \citep{RAGAS}.

RAGAS provides a scalable, reference-free framework for evaluating \ac{RAG} systems using three core dimensions: faithfulness, answer relevance, and context relevance \citep{RAGAS}. This decomposition is especially useful during system development, because it enables rapid comparison of \ac{RAG} systems even when human-annotated references are unavailable \citep{RAGAS}. ARES challenges the assumption that prompt-based judge frameworks are automatically reliable, showing that evaluation improves when judges are tailored to the target setting and calibrated with a small human-annotated validation set \citep{saad-falconARESAutomatedEvaluation2024}. Automated metrics are therefore valuable for scalable iteration, but they still require scrutiny over judge design and validation before they should be trusted for strong comparative claims \citep{RAGAS,saad-falconARESAutomatedEvaluation2024}.

Even when automated frameworks report several quality dimensions, evaluation still remains too coarse if it only produces overall scores without showing where failure arose \citep{ruRAGCheckerFinegrainedFramework2024}. \citet{ruRAGCheckerFinegrainedFramework2024} therefore argue that \ac{RAG} evaluation should serve both user-facing comparison and developer-facing diagnosis, because errors may originate in retrieval, generation, or in the interaction between the two. RAGChecker extends this line of work by moving from response-level assessment to claim-level entailment checking and by separating overall metrics from diagnostic retriever and generator metrics \citep{ruRAGCheckerFinegrainedFramework2024}. This is particularly important for system development, since fine-grained evaluation can expose trade-offs that aggregate scores hide, such as retrieval improvements that also introduce noise or generators that appear strong overall but still produce unsupported claims \citep{ruRAGCheckerFinegrainedFramework2024}.

Metric choice alone does not make \ac{RAG} evaluation convincing. In particular, the benchmark must also expose the retrieval conditions under which the systems fail \citep{BenchmarkingLargeLanguage}. \citet{BenchmarkingLargeLanguage} address this directly in their \ac{RGB} benchmark by organising evaluation around four fundamental abilities for \ac{RAG}: noise robustness, negative rejection, information integration, and counterfactual robustness. This choice of evaluation cases is especially valuable since it tests not only whether a model can answer when helpful evidence is present, but also whether it can refuse when evidence is missing, combine evidence across documents, and resist misleading retrieved content \citep{BenchmarkingLargeLanguage}.

Taken together, the literature supports that strong \ac{RAG} evaluation should combine scalable automated scoring with smaller amounts of human-grounded validation and targeted diagnostic analysis, rather than relying on any single framework or headline metric alone \citep{RAGAS,saad-falconARESAutomatedEvaluation2024,ruRAGCheckerFinegrainedFramework2024}. Automated frameworks remain valuable because they make the iterative comparison of \ac{RAG} configurations practical at development time, especially when large-scale human annotation is unavailable \citep{RAGAS,saad-falconARESAutomatedEvaluation2024}. However, both the limitations of \ac{LLM}-as-a-judge methods and the importance of diagnostic analysis indicate that automated scores should be interpreted with caution and complemented by human judgement and failure analysis when drawing stronger comparative conclusions \citep{RAGAS,saad-falconARESAutomatedEvaluation2024,ruRAGCheckerFinegrainedFramework2024}. These studies justify the evaluation design developed in Chapter \ref{cha:methodology}, which uses automated metrics for scalable ablation and comparison, while retaining manual evaluation and error analysis to assess response quality and identify failure modes.

\section{Gap Analysis and Research Positioning}
\label{sec:gap}

The reviewed literature positions \ac{HPC} support as a specialised retrieval-grounded \ac{QA} problem rather than a generic chatbot task \citep{AskHPC,yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. AskHPC show that \ac{HPC} support information is scattered across multiple sources \citep{AskHPC}, while \citet{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021} frame technical support \ac{QA} as a retrieve-then-read process in which relevant evidence must be found before an answer can be identified. This is important because answer quality depends on appropriate external evidence, while technical-support datasets are often limited by the cost of expert annotation \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}.

This chapter has also shown that the support evidence base is heterogeneous rather than uniform, with each source contributing a different type of information \citep{AskHPC,rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025}. In particular, local documentation, external technical documentation, and historical support tickets provide current site-specific guidance, supplementary software detail, and prior issue-resolution experience respectively \citep{AskHPC,rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025}. Furthermore, since these sources differ in authority, temporal stability, and local applicability, they cannot be treated as interchangeable \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025}.

The heterogeneity of the underlying support corpus exposes a limitation in baseline \ac{RAG} architectures. Although baseline \ac{RAG} improves knowledge-intensive generation by grounding outputs in external knowledge \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}, later work shows that reliable \ac{RAG} also depends on whether retrieved evidence is in scope, current, and trustworthy \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025}. In particular, \citet{liYouKnowWhat2024} show that degradation occurs when the underlying corpus is out of scope or out of date, \citet{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025} show that relevance-only retrieval overlooks source reliability, and \citep{ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025} show that outdated evidence can reduce accuracy and mislead generation even when valid evidence is present. 

The resulting literature gap is therefore not that \ac{HPC} support has never been addressed using retrieval-augmented generation. AskHPC, for example, demonstrates the value of documentation-grounded \ac{RAG} for HPC user support, and ticket-focused studies highlight the value of historical support interactions \citep{AskHPC,rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025}. This review did not identify prior work that jointly evaluates retrieval-time evidence prioritisation across documentation and tickets when those sources differ in freshness, authority, and local validity \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025}. More specifically, this review did not identify prior work that tests whether answer quality improves when heterogeneous support sources are combined with validity-aware reranking rather than source-agnostic semantic retrieval.

The dissertation is therefore positioned against a narrow gap defined by the intersection of heterogeneous support evidence, validity-aware prioritisation, and supervised first-response assistance for \ac{CSD3}. Within this scope, the project tests whether combining documentation and tickets improves factual correctness over single-source baselines, and whether validity-aware reranking improves over naive combined retrieval. 

The literature further implies that evaluation must mirror the structure of the problem. \citet{RAGAS} argue that \ac{RAG} quality cannot be reduced to a single end-to-end score, while \citet{ruRAGCheckerFinegrainedFramework2024} show that useful diagnosis requires separating retriever and generator failure modes. As a result, the later chapters evaluate retrieval quality, faithfulness, factual correctness, and error patterns rather than relying on one headline metric.
