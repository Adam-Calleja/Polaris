\chapter{Background and Related Work}
\label{cha:background}

This chapter introduces the relevant background and related work for this project, structured around its key design decisions. It starts by framing HPC support as an information retrieval and question answering problem, then introduces the concept of \ac{RAG} and its relevance to this domain. The chapter also discusses the heterogeneous nature of support corpora, including documentation and tickets, and the challenges of authority, freshness, and validity in retrieval. Finally, it reviews existing evaluation methodologies for RAG systems, identifies common failure modes of naive retrieval approaches, and positions this research within the broader landscape of related work.

\section{HPC Support as an Information Retrieval and Question Answering Problem}
\label{sec:support}

Current user support for HPC systems such as \ac{CSD3} is typically delivered through a combination of documentation, helpdesk systems, and direct communication with support staff and \ac{HPC} experts \citep{AskHPC}. This support ecosystem is underpinned by a large body of user documentation, which serves as a first point of reference for users and helps them solve problems independently. However, much of this essential information is scattered across fragmented, multimodal sources, making it difficult and time-consuming to locate. \citet{AskHPC} therefore argue that as \ac{HPC} systems and software stacks continue to grow in complexity, it becomes increasingly difficult for even experienced users to navigate this documentation effectively, creating barriers to usability and causing frustration, errors, and reduced productivity \citep{AskHPC}. This is particularly important in the context of \ac{HPC} support, where incorrect guidance can result in misconfigurations, failed computations, wasted computational resources, or even violations of facility policies \citep{AskHPC}. 

These characteristics make \ac{HPC} support a natural \ac{IR} and \ac{QA} problem \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,AskHPC}. In the technical support setting, answering a user's query is a two-stage process: the system must first retrieve the relevant documents or support artefacts, and the retrieved information must then be read to identify the answer \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. Specifically, the retrieved information must not only be semantically similar to the user's query, but it must also contain the required information to answer the question \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. This is particularly important for procedural and troubleshooting questions, for which the answer is non-factoid and often requires actionable guidance rather than a memorised fact \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}. The task is further complicated by the fact that the required documentation may not exist, and even if it does, it may be difficult to find in a fragmented, multimodal documentation environment \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,AskHPC}. 

Technical support \ac{QA} differs from general open-domain \ac{QA} in several key ways \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. First, it is grounded in real user queries that use specialised domain language and often refer to specific software versions, configurations, and snippets of stack traces \citep{castelliTechQADataset2020}. Second, \citet{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021} argue that, in contrast to general open-domain \ac{QA}, technical support \ac{QA} often contains a weak lexical overlap between the question and the answer. Therefore, documents that appear semantically relevant may fail to contain the actual resolution. Straightforward retrieve-then-read pipelines may return such documents, making them more error-prone in technical settings \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021}. Third, technical \ac{QA} is constrained by relatively small labelled datasets, since annotation requires domain expertise and is significantly more expensive than crowd-sourced open-domain annotation \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}. Taken together, these characteristics make direct transfer from methods trained on open-domain corpora more difficult and can lead to weaker performance in technical settings \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}.

In addition to official documentation, historical support tickets provide another source of operational support knowledge in \ac{HPC} settings \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. TicketHub shows that historical support tickets can help staff identify prior issues and resolutions, improving the efficiency of support responses \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. The authors also show that such tickets play an important role in preserving institutional knowledge, which is particularly valuable when new staff face a steep learning curve or when ticketing systems change. Therefore, \ac{HPC} support depends not only on access to documentation, but also on access to prior support interactions and their resolutions \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. 

Taken together, the literature indicates that \ac{HPC} support is best understood as a specialised, evidence-dependent form of technical support \ac{QA} rather than as a general instance of open-domain factoid \ac{QA} \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020,AskHPC}. 

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Generation}
\label{sec:rag}

As discussed in \ref{sec:support}, technical support \ac{QA} is evidence-dependent: answering a query requires retrieving the relevant external information and identifying an answer within it \citep{yuTechnicalQuestionAnswering2021,castelliTechQADataset2020}. For this reason, parametric-only \ac{LM}s are a weak fit for knowledge-intensive support tasks, because they rely on knowledge stored in model parameters rather than directly verifiable external evidence \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}. As \citet{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020} note, such models are difficult to update, provide limited provenance for their predictions, and can hallucinate on knowledge-intensive tasks. Retrieval-augmented generation (\acs{RAG}) addresses this limitation by combining language generation with retrieval from an external knowledge source \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}.

\citet{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020} define \ac{RAG} as a hybrid architecture that combines parametric memory in a pre-trained sequence-to-sequence generator model with non-parametric memory in a dense document index. At inference time, a neural retriever selects the most relevant passages for a given input query and the generator produces a response using these passages as additional context. \ac{RAG} is therefore a retrieval-grounded generation framework intended to improve factual performance while making the evidential basis of an answer more inspectable \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}.

\citet{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020} report strong empirical results on open-domain \ac{QA}, knowledge-intensive generation, and fact verification benchmarks. Their models outperform parametric-only baselines on several open-domain \ac{QA} tasks and generate more factual, specific, and diverse responses than BART in generation settings \citep{lewisRetrievalaugmentedGenerationKnowledgeintensive2020}. Additionally, because knowledge is stored partly in an external document index, that knowledge can be updated by replacing or rebuilding the index rather than retraining the generator. Related work further shows that retrieval-augmented generation can also reduce hallucination in grounded conversation tasks \citep{shusterRetrievalAugmentationReduces2021}.

However, the original \ac{RAG} architecture does not by itself ensure that retrieval is necessary, that the retrieved material is trustworthy, or that the generator will faithfully use it in the final answer \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. \citet{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023} argue that fixed-$k$ retrieval can be wasteful or harmful when retrieval is unnecessary or when the retrieved passages are weakly grounded. \citet{liYouKnowWhat2024} show that \ac{RAG} quality depends on query-knowledge relevance, and that it degrades when the underlying corpus is out of scope or out of date. \citet{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025} further show that relevance alone is insufficient in multi-source settings, because source reliability affects whether retrieved evidence should be trusted. For technical support, this means that semantically relevant material may still be unusable if it is outdated or if it comes from a less reliable source \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}.

Taken together, these studies shift the design problem from simply adding a retrieval component to controlling when retrieval is used and which evidence is prioritised \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. Self-RAG is one response to this problem. \citet{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023} propose a framework that allows retrieval on demand and uses reflection tokens to assess the relevance of retrieved passages and whether the generated content is supported by the retrieved evidence.

Applied support-orientated \ac{RAG} systems also suggest that retrieval design remains domain-specific. AskHPC combines curated \ac{HPC} documentation with specialised parsing \citep{AskHPC}, while customer-service systems have incorporated structured knowledge graphs to improve retrieval \citep{LinkedInRAG}. For \ac{HPC} support, the implication is that a useful \ac{RAG} system must not only retrieve semantically relevant information, but it must also prioritise evidence that is current, trustworthy, and operationally valid \citep{AskHPC,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. This motivates this project's focus on validity-aware retrieval over heterogeneous support evidence, rather on generation alone. 

\section{Heterogeneous Support Corpora: Documentation and Tickets}
\label{sec:heterogeneous}

\ac{HPC} user support draws on a heterogeneous evidence base that includes local facility documentation, external technical documentation, and historical support tickets rather than a single uniform corpus \citep{AskHPC,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025,rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}. In this context, heterogeneity means that these sources differ not only in topic coverage, but also in their evidential role, authority, update cycle, and temporal stability \citep{AskHPC,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. In multi-source retrieval settings, these differences affect whether apparently relevant evidence is still trustworthy, locally applicable, and suitable for the current system state \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. Source selection is therefore an important part of the retrieval problem \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. For facility-specific support, local official documentation is the primary documentary source because it records the workflows, constraints, and configurations that are actually supported on the target system \citep{AskHPC,ResearchComputingServicesDocumentation}. AskHPC describes user documentation as the first point of reference for \ac{HPC} users, which makes the quality, coverage, and maintenance of local documentation central to scalable support \citep{AskHPC}. In the case of \ac{CSD3}, the local documentation covers access, authentication, job submission, software environments, and site policies \citep{ResearchComputingServicesDocumentation}. It is therefore the best available source for questions about permitted or supported local practice. 

External technical documentation has a complementary evidential role since it can extend local support coverage without replacing local authority \citep{AskHPC,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. It is most useful when a query depends on tool behaviour, syntax, API usage, or configuration detail that local documentation omits, summarises only briefly, or abstracts away \citep{AskHPC}. \citet{AskHPC} illustrate this multi-source setting clearly by combining facility guides with scheduler manuals and programming-model documentation to answer queries whose evidence requirements span multiple sources. However, general software documentation cannot by itself establish local operational truth, because current local practice may differ from the general case \citep{AskHPC,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. As a result, this project treats external documentation as supplementary evidence for \ac{CSD3}-specific queries rather than as the deciding source on local policy, permissions, or configuration.

Historical support tickets perform a different evidential role to documentation, because they preserve concrete user problems, diagnostic context, troubleshooting paths, and prior resolutions in a way that formal documentation often does not \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025}. \citet{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023} show that archival ticket analysis can help staff identify common issues, retrieve previous resolutions, and reduce the steep learning curve faced by new staff by making prior cases easier to search. \citet{joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025} further show that \ac{HPC} tickets can be normalised into structured issue-resolution pairs and used to generate FAQ-style support knowledge from recurring support patterns. They also note that ticket corpora are difficult to prepare cleanly because they contain domain-specific terminology, cryptic error messages, inconsistent wording, and user-specific artefacts, all of which complicate normalisation, summarisation, and retrieval. Tickets are also a risky evidence source because they are a lagging indicator of system state. While a closed ticket may preserve a useful resolution, that resolution may no longer be valid after later changes to documentation, software versions, or local policy \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,liYouKnowWhat2024}. \citet{joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025} additionally highlight the privacy and governance constraints of ticket data, since personally identifying and user-specific content may persist even after redaction. This means that ticket evidence often requires stronger access control and more careful preprocessing than documentation sources \cite{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023}.

Taken together, the literature supports modelling \ac{HPC} support evidence as a source-aware retrieval setting rather than as a single undifferentiated corpus, because the source type changes both the evidential value and the retrieval risk \citep{AskHPC,rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025,liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. For this project, this means indexing and ranking account for source type, local applicability, freshness, and evidential risk alongside semantic relevance \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. This motivates the validity-aware retrieval design developed in the next section.  

\section{Authority, Freshness, and Validity in Retrieval}
\label{sec:authority-freshness-validity}

Accordingly, the central retrieval question in this project is not simply whether a passage is semantically related to a query, but whether it should be treated as usable evidence for an answer \citep{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,liYouKnowWhat2024}. In a multi-source support corpus, relevance-only retrieval is inadequate because a highly relevant document may come from an unreliable source, or a seemingly appropriate corpus may still fail if the query lies beyond its effective scope or depends on outdated knowledge \cite{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,liYouKnowWhat2024}. This is especially problematic in operational support settings, where outdated information can reduce response accuracy and even steer generation towards harmful answers despite the simultaneous presence of current evidence \citep{ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025}. In this section, authority refers to the trustworthiness of a source, freshness to the temporal currency of its information, and validity to whether retrieved evidence is suitable for the current local support context.

Authority becomes importance once the retrieval corpus varies in evidential trustworthiness rather than topic coverage only \citep{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}. \citet{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025} show that standard multi-source \ac{RAG} can retrieve incorrect information because it relies on relevance alone while overlooking source reliability. This can allow unreliable sources to dominate the retrieval process. As discussed in Section \ref{sec:heterogeneous}, this is especially important for operational support questions about local policy, permissions, and supported configuration, where generic software documentation cannot by itself establish local operational truth. Authority is therefore a retrieval problem, not just a post-hoc verification problem: if authoritative evidence is not prioritised before generation, the model may still ground its answer is semantically relevant but less trustworthy material \citep{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025}.

Freshness is important because the evidential value of a retrieved document can decay over time, even when its semantic relevance remains high \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025}. \citet{liYouKnowWhat2024} show that \ac{RAG} performance degrades when the underlying corpus is out of data or no longer adequately supports the active query distribution. \citet{ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025} further demonstrate that the presence of outdated information in retrieved context can substantially reduce answer accuracy despite the simultaneous presence of current evidence. In \ac{HPC} support, this temporal risk is amplified because the operational environment itself changes over time, as software stacks, documentation, policies, and even the available hardware evolve, meaning that older resolutions may no longer reflect the current system state \citep{rodenbeckTicketHubEnablingActionable2023,joslinGeneratingFrequentlyAsked2025,CSD3UpgradeOctober}. Therefore, freshness is not only a useful ranking heuristic, but also a condition on whether retrieved evidence can still be safely used to answer present-focused operational queries \citep{liYouKnowWhat2024,ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025}.

In this dissertation, validity is used as an operational term for retrieved evidence that is not only semantically relevant, but also authoritative, current, and locally applicable. This means that a passage can be topically relevant yet still be invalid if it comes from a less trustworthy source, reflects an outdated system state, or does not establish the correct local operational truth \citep{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,liYouKnowWhat2024}. This suggests that temporal validity should not be reduced to a simple age threshold alone, since the usability of support evidence depends on whether it still reflects the current local operational context. It also explains why indiscriminate fixed-$k$ retrieval is risky in this setting: adding more semantically related context does not guarantee better grounding, and the generator is not guaranteed to follow the retrieved evidence faithfully \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023}. Validity-aware retrieval should therefore prefer evidence that is jointly relevant, reliable, current, and applicable to the local support context, rather than treating semantic similarity alone as a sufficient ranking criteria \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023,hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,liYouKnowWhat2024}.

Taken together, these studies imply that retrieval for HPC support should not be organised as a relevance-only search over a flattened corpus. Instead, it should incorporate source-aware preferences, freshness-sensitive controls, and local applicability constraints so that generation is grounded primarily in evidence that is defensible in the current support context \citep{hwangRetrievalAugmentedGenerationEstimation2025,liYouKnowWhat2024,ouyangHoHDynamicBenchmark2025}. The studies also suggest that when retrieved evidence is weak, stale, or conflicting, forcing a fully resolved answer is riskier than responding conservatively or exposing uncertainty \citep{asaiSelfRAGLearningRetrieve2023,liYouKnowWhat2024}. Therefore, this project treats validity-aware retrieval as a prerequisite for trustworthy support generation, which motivates the retrieval and reranking design developed in Chapter \ref{cha:system-design-implementation}.

\section{Evaluation of RAG Systems}
\label{sec:evaluation}

Evaluating \ac{RAG} systems is a more complex problem than evaluating standalone \ac{LM}s because system quality depends not only on the final answer, but also on whether the retriever finds suitable evidence and whether the generator uses that evidence faithfully \citep{RAGAS,ruRAGCheckerFinegrainedFramework2024}. A single end-to-end score is therefore insufficient on its own since the a poor final answer could be cause by retrieval failure, incomplete evidence, or weak use of otherwise valid context \cite{ruRAGCheckerFinegrainedFramework2024}. As a result, recent work treats \ac{RAG} evaluation as a multi-dimensional problem that separates retrieval quality from grounded generation quality \cite{saad-falconARESAutomatedEvaluation2024,RAGAS}. This is typically operationalised through measures such as context relevance, answer faithfulness, and answer relevance.
